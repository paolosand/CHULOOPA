  \begin{abstract}
    Looping systems are inherently repetitive --- the same pattern cycles indefinitely, limiting the musical dynamics of a live performance. CHULOOPA is a dynamic drum looper that uses beatbox input to create patterns, then autonomously varies them based on the performer's live musical energy, or ``spice'' --- a composite audio feature derived from spectral flux, RMS energy, and onset density. At loop boundaries, one of 5 pre-generated variations (from rhythmic\_creator, a hybrid transformer based model drum pattern generator) is selected to match the current spice level; continuation-based generation preserves non-quantized timing, maintaining human groove across variations. Variations span a spectrum from subtle to experimental (spice 0.2--1.0), generated offline on consumer hardware. CHULOOPA demonstrates that a looper can be musically consistent yet dynamically responsive.
  \end{abstract}

  \textbf{Keywords:} dynamic looping, audio-driven variation, spice detection, timing preservation, co-creative AI, live performance
]

\section{INTRODUCTION}

As generative AI systems increasingly mediate musical creation, questions arise about agency, accessibility, and creative control. Most AI music tools demand either technical expertise (programming, music theory) or reliance on cloud platforms (API dependencies, data privacy concerns). We ask: \emph{Can AI-mediated musicianship be accessible, personalized, and preserve human expression?}

CHULOOPA (ChucK-based User-trainable Loop Operator for Performance Audio \citep{wang2015chuck}) addresses this through \textbf{personalization-over-scale}: rather than training generic models on massive datasets, we create user-trainable tools that adapt to individual creative practices. A solo performer beatboxes 10 samples each of kick, snare, and hi-hat---their own idiosyncratic sounds---and the system learns \emph{their} vocabulary. This challenges the assumption that music AI requires extensive training data \citep{weber2024realtime}.

\subsection{Novel Contributions}

\textbf{1. Minimal-data personalization:} 10 user-specific samples per class achieve \textbf{$\sim$(COMPUTE FOR ACTUAL ACCURACY)\%} accuracy for amateur beatbox classification, demonstrating that constraining the problem space to one user's style enables high accuracy without large datasets.

\textbf{2. Timing-preserving AI variation:} Continuation-based variation generation using a local transformer-LSTM preserves non-quantized timing, maintaining human ``feel'' while introducing musical diversity.

\textbf{3. Mediated musicianship design:} Audio-driven spice detection, pre-generated 5-variant banks, queued actions at loop boundaries, and offline-first architecture keep the artist ``in the loop,'' exemplifying distributed agency between human and AI.

\subsection{Alignment with Conference Theme}

CHULOOPA engages with \emph{mediated musicianship in a hyper-reproductive age} by:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Challenging hyper-reproduction}: Local AI inference (no cloud platforms) resists centralized generative infrastructures
\item \textbf{Responsive AI}: Audio-driven spice detection reads live musical energy and selects variations accordingly; the performer sets a ceiling rather than issuing manual commands
\item \textbf{Distributed agency}: Human beatbox input + AI variation = collaborative authorship
\item \textbf{Accessibility}: Voice interface and minimal training (5 minutes) lower barriers to AI-mediated creation
\end{itemize}


\section{RELATED WORK}

\textbf{Beatbox Recognition.} \citet{stowell2010beatbox} achieved high accuracy for professional beatboxers using large datasets. \citet{delgado2019dataset} showed amateur beatboxers are inconsistent \emph{across} performers but consistent \emph{within} their own styles---validating our personalization approach. Recent work \citep{martanto2025beatbox} confirms KNN achieves 94.55\% accuracy for beatbox with optimal features.

\textbf{Personalized ML for Music.} Few-shot learning for audio has shown promise \citep{wang2020metaaudio,pons2019prototypical}. \citet{weber2024realtime} demonstrated real-time drum transcription using dynamic few-shot learning, validating minimal-data approaches for performance contexts. Our work extends this to accessible creative tools: users simply beatbox 10 samples through an interactive recorder.

\textbf{AI Music Generation.} Transformer-based rhythm generation \citep{huang2018music,shih2023theme} typically requires quantized input. \citet{gillick2019learning} explored groove with GrooVAE but forces 16th-note grids. We adapt \citet{chen2025music}'s rhythmic\_creator (transformer-LSTM trained on 13,000+ MIDI sequences) by extracting its \emph{continuation} output and applying proportional time-warping to preserve non-quantized timing. \citet{jonason2025smart} show that user-facing control over generation quality involves diversity-quality tradeoffs, validating CHULOOPA's post-processing spice approach. \citet{anoufa2025bass} demonstrate a parallel conditional generation task (bass from rhythm guitar) using a transformer-decoder, confirming this architecture generalizes across instrument accompaniment domains.

\textbf{Co-Creative AI.} The AIMC 2025 theme ``The Artist in the Loop'' emphasizes human agency. \citet{wilson2025responsible} survey AI music tools through Responsible AI principles, identifying a lack of real-time performance-oriented systems---a gap CHULOOPA fills. \citet{loughran2020human} identified key principles: transparency, agency, creative dialogue. \citet{shepardson2023living} explored AI-augmented looping with RAVE autoencoders. \citet{lee2025revival} demonstrate AI musical agents trained on small curated corpora in live performance, validating CHULOOPA's small-data approach. CHULOOPA contributes real-time creativity control (spice level) and offline-first reliability for live performance.

\textbf{Gap.} No existing system combines user-trainable personalization (10 samples/class), real-time transcription ($<$50ms), offline AI variation (local transformer-LSTM), timing preservation (non-quantized), and live performance integration in an accessible end-to-end instrument.

\subsection{Personalized Training Pipeline}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{images/drum_sample_recorder_ui.png}
  \caption{Training UI (\texttt{drum\_sample\_recorder.ck}). Three shapes (cube = kick, octahedron = snare, dodecahedron = hi-hat) pulse on each sample and grow brighter as training data accumulates. Press K/S/H to label, E to export.}
  \label{fig:training_ui}
\end{figure}

Users run \texttt{drum\_sample\_recorder.ck} and record 10 one-second samples each of kick, snare, and hi-hat sounds. The system extracts \textbf{13 MFCC coefficients} per onset---the standard speech feature range validated by \citet{martanto2025beatbox}, who showed MFCCs outperform spectral band energies for voice-derived percussion because beatbox sounds ``are better identified when using a feature more commonly used in identifying the human voice.''

Features are saved to \texttt{training\_samples.csv}. When CHULOOPA starts, a KNN classifier (k=3) trains automatically in $<$1 second. KNN was chosen over deep learning because: (1) performs well with 30 total samples, (2) no overfitting, (3) instant training, (4) interpretable for debugging.

\textbf{Why minimal data works:} User-specific training constrains the problem space to one person's vocal style. Amateur beatboxers are remarkably consistent in their own imperfect technique \citep{delgado2019dataset}. KNN's instance-based learning handles small datasets without parametric assumptions.

\subsection{Real-Time Transcription}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{images/chuloopa_active_state.png}
  \caption{CHULOOPA performance UI (\texttt{chuloopa\_main.ck}) during active variation playback. Shape color tracks spice level (blue = low, yellow = mid, red = high). The spice slider (bottom left) shows audio-driven spice; state text (bottom right) displays current mode. Shapes pulse on each drum hit.}
  \label{fig:active_ui}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Onset Detection:} Spectral flux with adaptive thresholding (1.5$\times$ running mean) in 512-sample frames, 128-sample hop (75\% overlap). The 512-sample frame provides 86 Hz frequency resolution at 44.1 kHz---necessary to discriminate kick fundamentals ($\sim$60-100Hz) from mid-frequency snare energy. Smaller windows would degrade classification accuracy \citep{bello2005tutorial}.

\textbf{Classification \& Playback:} Each onset is classified (kick/snare/hat) and corresponding drum samples play immediately. Total latency: $\sim$25ms (perceptually acceptable for live performance). Timing, drum class, and velocity are stored with delta-time encoding for drift-free loop playback.

\subsection{AI Variation Generation}

When a new pattern is recorded, a Python background process (\texttt{drum\_variation\_generator.py}) immediately generates a \textbf{bank of 5 variations in parallel} at spice levels 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8, and 1.0. Spice maps to a \emph{token count ceiling}: how many tokens rhythmic\_creator generates beyond the original context (max 3$\times$). Higher spice = more continuation tokens = greater divergence from the original pattern.

Each variant is generated via:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Feed original pattern as context to transformer-LSTM
\item Generate continuation up to spice-determined token ceiling
\item Proportional time-warp to match original loop duration: $t_{new} = t_{AI} \times \frac{duration_{original}}{duration_{AI}}$
\end{enumerate}

Once all 5 variants are ready, Python sends \texttt{/chuloopa/bank\_ready} via OSC. A second process, \textbf{\texttt{spice\_detector.ck}}, continuously analyzes live audio and streams a composite spice value to \texttt{chuloopa\_main.ck} every 500ms. At each loop boundary, ChucK selects the variant whose spice level best matches the \emph{rolling 4-bar spice average}. The performer uses \textbf{CC 74} to set a \emph{ceiling} on how high the audio-driven spice can reach, maintaining creative control without requiring manual variation selection.

\textbf{Using Gemini (optional):} A cloud alternative (\texttt{drum\_variation\_gemini.py}) maps spice directly to sampling temperature via the Gemini API.

\textbf{Performance:} 3-5s total for all 5 variants (rhythmic\_creator, CPU only). Entirely offline by default.

\subsection{Queued Actions for Live Performance}

Inspired by hardware loopers, actions (clear track, bank regeneration) queue during loop cycles and execute at loop boundaries. Each playback session has a unique ID; scheduled drum hits check their session ID before playing, preventing overlap. This ensures musically-coherent transitions without glitches.

\textbf{MIDI Controls:} Note 36 (hold) = record; Note 37 = clear; Note 38 = regenerate full variation bank; CC 74 = spice ceiling (caps audio-driven selection); CC 75 = guitar/vocal input mix (requires 2-channel audio interface). Variation selection is automatic---no manual toggle required.


\section{EVALUATION}

\subsection{Technical Performance}

\textbf{Classifier Accuracy:} Preliminary testing shows $\sim$(COMPUTE FOR ACTUAL ACCURACY)\% accuracy on user-specific amateur beatbox samples (formal evaluation with confusion matrix in progress).

\textbf{Latency:} Total input-to-output latency measured at $\sim$25ms (onset detection 11.6ms + hop 2.9ms + feature extraction 3-5ms + KNN 1-2ms). This falls within acceptable range for live musical performance (10-30ms threshold) \citep{wyse2018realtime}.

\textbf{Timing Preservation:} AI-generated variations maintain exact loop duration (bit-perfect match) while preserving proportional spacing between hits. Informal listening tests confirm non-quantized ``feel'' is maintained.

\subsection{Autoethnographic Findings}

As designer and primary user, I documented practice sessions over 3 weeks. Key findings:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Training accessibility:} 5-minute training session achieves usable accuracy for solo practice
\item \textbf{Real-time feedback value:} Hearing drum samples during beatboxing enables technique correction mid-performance
\item \textbf{Variation quality:} Continuation-based variations sound musically coherent; audio-driven spice selection provides hands-free creative evolution without interrupting performance flow
\item \textbf{Latency perception:} 25ms lag is perceivable during fast sequences but does not prevent successful pattern recording
\end{itemize}

\section{DISCUSSION}

\subsection{Personalization-Over-Scale}

Conventional ML wisdom suggests more data is always better. CHULOOPA demonstrates an exception: \textbf{for creative tools designed around individual practice, less data from the right user outperforms more data from many users}. Three factors explain why 10 samples achieve $\sim$(COMPUTE FOR ACTUAL ACCURACY)\% accuracy: (1) consistency within idiosyncrasy---amateurs are consistent in their \emph{own} style, (2) constrained problem space eliminates inter-user variability, (3) KNN's suitability for small data without overfitting.

\textbf{Implication:} Accessible creative AI might prioritize \emph{easily personalizable} models over pre-trained generic systems.

\subsection{Mediated Musicianship and Distributed Agency}

CHULOOPA exemplifies distributed agency: the performer provides rhythmic ideas through beatbox, AI extends and varies these ideas, and the system responds to the musical energy of the live performance. Rather than requiring manual variation selection, audio-driven spice detection reads the sonic environment and selects increasingly adventurous variants as musical intensity rises. The performer maintains agency through CC 74 (spice ceiling)---setting a maximum creative latitude without micromanaging individual transitions. This differs from autonomous generation (AI replaces human) or passive consumption (human accepts AI output without modification). The ceiling-plus-automation model keeps the artist ``in the loop''---a design principle for co-creative systems \citep{lubart2005creativity}.

The continuation-based approach preserves the performer's timing ``fingerprint'' (non-quantized groove), positioning AI as \emph{augmentation} rather than \emph{replacement}. This challenges quantization-based variation systems that destroy expressive microtiming \citep{davies2013microtiming,kilchenmann2015microtiming}.

\subsection{Offline-First as Resistance to Platform Infrastructures}

Running entirely on consumer hardware without cloud APIs represents a design stance: reliability and privacy over platform convenience. Local transformer-LSTM inference (4.5M parameters, CPU-only) demonstrates that sophisticated AI-mediated musicianship need not depend on proprietary platforms. This aligns with critical perspectives on generative AI's consolidation within centralized infrastructures.

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{(1) Variable variation density:} Continuation-based approach produces variable hit counts across the 5 variants; the most divergent (spice 1.0) may sound disconnected from the original. \textbf{(2) Single-track:} Multi-track requires sync coordination. \textbf{(3) $\sim$10\% errors:} Kick$\leftrightarrow$snare confusion when amateurs don't differentiate frequency content. \textbf{(4) Amateur-focused:} Professionals are underserved by 3-class simplification. \textbf{(5) Spice detection latency:} Audio spice updates every 500ms; fast musical transitions may not be reflected immediately in variation selection.


\section{FUTURE WORK}

\textbf{Broader Vision:} The current drum system represents a proof-of-concept for a complete voice-controlled looping ecosystem. Future development aims to enable: (1) multi-track looping (drums, bass, melody) with per-track AI variation, (2) multi-domain AI generation extending timing preservation to melodic content, (3) performance-responsive AI that listens to live input to guide variations, and (4) voice-to-instrument synthesis allowing users to perform vocally then swap to other instruments while preserving expressive timing. Offline-first architecture remains central to this vision.

\textbf{Immediate steps:} Multi-track support (3 tracks with per-track spice control and independent variation banks), fine-tuning rhythmic\_creator on drum-only dataset for improved continuation quality, and longitudinal study (3-6 months) with solo performers to evaluate audio-driven spice responsiveness in real performance settings.


\section{CONCLUSION}

CHULOOPA demonstrates three key insights for mediated musicianship in the generative AI era:

\textbf{1. Personalization enables accessibility:} Training on 10 user-specific samples achieves high accuracy without large datasets or technical expertise. Voice interface meets users where their skills already are.

\textbf{2. Timing preservation respects human expression:} Continuation-based variation with proportional time-warping maintains non-quantized groove, showing AI can augment creativity without destroying expressive microtiming.

\textbf{3. Offline-first resists platform dependence:} Local transformer-LSTM inference (3-5s, CPU-only) provides sophisticated AI variation without cloud APIs, demonstrating that mediated musicianship need not consolidate within proprietary infrastructures.

By treating amateur beatboxing---imperfect, idiosyncratic, personal---as legitimate musical interface, we expand who can participate in AI-mediated creation. The system positions AI as \emph{personal drum machine}: learning your beatbox vocabulary, preserving your rhythmic feel, extending your creative ideas.

\textbf{The future of accessible music AI may not be bigger models trained on more data, but smaller models trained on \emph{your} data.}
